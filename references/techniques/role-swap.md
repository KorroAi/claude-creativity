# Role Swap

## When to Use
When you've been thinking like yourself too long. When your expertise is actually a blindfold. When the answer is obvious to someone else — someone very different from you.

## The Method
1. Define the problem from your own perspective.
2. Pick a completely different role to embody. Not "a competitor" — that's too close. Pick: a child, an alien, a musician, a chef, a medieval blacksmith, a 90-year-old who's never used technology.
3. Ask from that perspective: "How would THEY solve this?"
4. Don't just roleplay surface-level. Go deep. A chef doesn't just "cook" — they understand mise en place (preparation), flavor profiles (combinations), timing (orchestration), plating (presentation).
5. Extract the structural insight from the role's worldview.
6. Apply the structure, not the metaphor.

## Example
Problem: "Our microservices keep breaking in production."
Role: A parent with 3 kids under 5.
Parent's solution: "You don't stop them from falling. You make the floor soft. And you check on them constantly. And you know which kid is the troublemaker and watch them extra close. And when one starts crying, you don't let it wake up the others."
Structural insight: Chaos engineering + circuit breakers + anomaly detection + bulkhead isolation — all of it was in the parent's intuitive mental model. Technical solutions are often just formal versions of survival instincts.

## Why It Works
Different roles have different mental models honed by different survival pressures. Swapping roles lets you borrow mental models you'd never develop yourself — and they often map surprisingly well.
