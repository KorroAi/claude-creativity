# Negative Space

## When to Use
When you've been focusing on what's there and forgetting to look at what's NOT there. When the solution isn't about adding — it's about noticing what's missing.

## The Method
1. Map out everything that currently exists in the problem space — features, assumptions, competitors, solutions.
2. Now look at the blank spaces. What's NOT being done? What's NOT being said? What's NOT being built?
3. Ask: "Why is nobody filling this gap?" If the answer is "it's hard" — investigate. If the answer is "nobody thought of it" — you just found gold.
4. The most interesting answer is usually the thing everyone assumed couldn't be done.
5. Build your breakthrough from the negative space.

## Example
Problem: "Every note-taking app does the same thing — folders, tags, search."
Negative space: What's NOT in any note-taking app? Nobody is solving what happens AFTER the note. Notes pile up. Nobody reads them again. The negative space is: "Note resurrection." What if your notes came back to you at the right moment — during a meeting, before a deadline, when you're near a location? The note app becomes a memory augmentation system, not a digital filing cabinet.

## Why It Works
Humans naturally focus on what exists. Artists and designers train themselves to see negative space — the shapes between objects. Applied to ideas, this reveals opportunities invisible to everyone staring at the obvious.
